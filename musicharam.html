The Hadith of Permissibility

Sahih al-Bukhari (5590)

"Among my ummah there will be people who will regard as permissible adultery, silk, alcohol and musical instruments."

Translation & Meaning: The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ warned his community that some people in the future would falsely claim that things explicitly forbidden—including musical instruments—are permissible. This is not a permitting of these acts, but rather a prediction and warning against their false permissibility.

Narrated by al-Bukhari. See also: al-Silsilah al-Saheehah by al-Albaani (91).

The Interpretation of Shaykh al-Islam Ibn Taymiyyah

Majmoo' al-Fataawa (11/535)

"This hadeeth indicates that musical instruments are haraam; this includes all types of instruments."

Meaning: Ibn Taymiyyah definitively interprets the above hadith to mean that all categories of musical instruments are prohibited without exception. This is the literal meaning of the Prophet's warning.

The Practice of the Righteous Generations

Majmoo' al-Fataawa (10/77)

"It is well known that during the best three generations of which the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allaah be upon him) said: 'The best of generations are my generation among whom I was sent, and the one that follows them, and the one that follows them', neither in the Hijaz nor in Syria nor in Yemen nor in Iraq nor in Egypt nor in Khorasan was there anyone among the people of goodness and religious commitment who would gather with others to listen to this innovated type of singing for the purpose of purifying the hearts. Hence it was regarded as makrooh by the imams such as Imam Ahmad and others."

Meaning: Throughout the first three generations (the best in Islam), across the entire Muslim world, there was no scholar or righteous person who gathered to listen to music. This unanimous practice of the salaf (righteous predecessors) proves it is prohibited.

Al-Shaafa'i on the Innovation of Music

Majmoo' al-Fataawa (10/77) - Al-Shafi'i's Statement

"In Baghdad I left behind something that was innovated by the zindeeqs (heretics) who called it taghbeer (a type of singing) and diverted the people from the Qur'aan thereby."

Meaning: Even in the second century of Islam, music was recognized as an innovation introduced by heretics specifically designed to distract people from the Quran. Al-Shafi'i distanced himself from this clear corruption.

The Hierarchy of Prohibition - Ibn al-Qayyim

Ighaathat al-Lahfaan (1/228)

"If the zamr (a kind of pipe or flute), which is the least evil of musical instruments, is haraam, then what about those which are worse such as the 'oud (a stringed instrument), tambour (drum) and yaraa' (a reed instrument)? No one who has had the slightest whiff of knowledge should hesitate to regard them as haraam, and the least that can be said is that they are the symbol of corrupt evildoers and wine drinkers."

Meaning: Ibn al-Qayyim establishes a logical hierarchy: if the most seemingly harmless instrument (the zamr/flute) is prohibited, then certainly more elaborate instruments are more severely prohibited. Musical instruments are the defining characteristic of corrupt people and those immersed in forbidden pleasures.

This demonstrates the complete prohibition extends to all instruments without exception.
